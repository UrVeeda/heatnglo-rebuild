If you're specifying a fireplace for a new build or major renovation, the odds are you'll end up specifying gas. Direct-vent gas, specifically. The category has dominated residential fireplace installs for two decades, and the reason isn't aesthetics. It's the structural limits of the alternatives.

## Wood-burning is a chimney project

A real wood-burning fireplace requires a real chimney. That means structural framing through the roof, a flue liner that meets code, ongoing chimney maintenance, and ash management for the life of the home. In new construction, the chimney is a structural commitment. In renovation, it's often a non-starter.

## Vent-free has restrictions

Vent-free gas fireplaces don't require any venting at all, which sounds appealing until you find out they're prohibited in some states and municipalities, restricted in others, and not the right answer for projects where indoor air quality is part of the design brief.

## Direct-vent removes both constraints

Direct-vent gas fireplaces draw combustion air from outside through a co-axial pipe system, then exhaust through the same pipe assembly. No chimney. No combustion air drawn from indoor space. No restrictions in the same jurisdictions that limit vent-free units. The system runs sealed, the firebox glass stays clean, indoor air quality is unaffected.

That's why the Mezzo, the Cosmo, the 6K/8K Series Traditional, the True, and the Primo II are all direct-vent. The category lets the design lead.
