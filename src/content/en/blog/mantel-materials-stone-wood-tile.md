Stone, wood, tile. Every fireplace surround in the Heat & Glo lineup pairs with one of these three families. Picking the right one is partly aesthetic, partly engineering, and entirely about clearance-to-combustibles.

## Stone: any clearance, any aesthetic

Stone surrounds are the most permissive specification because most stone is non-combustible. Cast stone, natural stone slab, dry-laid fieldstone, quartzite, travertine, limestone. Any of these can sit close to the firebox opening within the limits the manufacturer's installation manual sets.

Best for: contemporary linear families (Mezzo, Cosmo, Primo II) where a single continuous stone slab from hearth to ceiling reinforces the horizontal proportion of the firebox.

## Wood: the clearance-critical option

Wood mantels are aesthetic gold and engineering minefields. The wrong species or the wrong distance from the firebox opening creates a real fire-safety problem. Heat & Glo's wood mantel collections are profile-engineered to specific clearance specs, which is why they pair cleanly with the firebox families they're sold against.

Best for: traditional families (6K/8K Series, True) where a corbeled wood mantel cap is structurally and aesthetically expected. Always confirm clearance-to-combustibles requirements with the installation manual before specifying any wood mantel.

## Tile: the contemporary middle ground

Tile sits between stone and wood in clearance flexibility. Most ceramic and porcelain tiles are non-combustible, but the substrate behind them might not be, and the grout joints can fail under extreme heat exposure if not specified correctly.

Best for: contemporary linear families where large-format porcelain in a continuous slab look anchors the wall without the cost of natural stone.

## The discipline that matters across all three

Whatever the material, confirm clearance requirements against the specific Heat & Glo unit's installation manual before final design. The numbers in the manual are non-negotiable.
