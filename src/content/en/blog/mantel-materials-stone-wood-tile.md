## Direct answer

The right mantel material for a fireplace depends on three things:

1. **Clearance-to-combustibles** -- how close to the firebox opening the material can legally and safely sit
2. **Firebox family** -- whether the unit is contemporary linear (Mezzo, Cosmo, Primo II) or traditional (6K/8K Series, True)
3. **Room aesthetic** -- whether the surrounding design is contemporary, transitional, or traditional

Stone is the most permissive because most stone is non-combustible. Wood is the most clearance-critical because it is combustible by definition. Tile sits between them: the tile face is typically non-combustible, but the substrate behind it may not be.

The numbers in your specific fireplace's installation manual are non-negotiable regardless of material choice.

## Stone mantels and surrounds

Stone is the specifier's most forgiving choice because non-combustible materials don't carry the same clearance-to-combustibles risk as wood. That doesn't mean there are no clearance requirements -- it means the requirements are focused on the mechanical and structural side rather than the fire-safety side.

### Natural stone slab

A single continuous stone slab from hearth to ceiling is the signature treatment for contemporary linear fireplaces. Honed limestone, travertine, quartzite, or basalt can run floor-to-ceiling as a single wall element with the [Mezzo](/fireplaces/mezzo/) or [Cosmo](/fireplaces/cosmo/) cut out of it.

The installation sequence matters: stone is templated after the firebox is set and the rough surround is confirmed. Template before fabrication. Fabrication before installation. Never assume the wall is perfectly plumb and square -- template it.

### Cast stone surround

The [Cast Stone Surround](/accessories/cast-stone-surround/) is engineered to pair with the 6K/8K Series and True. It provides the classical proportions of a masonry surround without the weight of a full masonry installation. Cast stone is non-combustible and dimensioned to the specific clearance requirements of the fireplace it's sold against.

This is the right specification when the room wants the traditional hearth aesthetic but the installation doesn't have the structural depth for a full masonry build.

### Dry-laid fieldstone

Lodge aesthetic, highest labor cost. Dry-laid fieldstone installations are spec'd into premium traditional builds in mountain and Pacific Northwest markets. The irregularity of natural stone reads warm against the controlled flame of the [True Indoor Gas Fireplace](/fireplaces/true/) or the 6K/8K Series.

The stone itself is non-combustible. The mortar used in wet-laid installations is non-combustible. Confirm that any backing or mesh used in the installation is also non-combustible.

## Tile mantels and surrounds

Tile is the middle ground in cost, clearance, and aesthetic range. Most ceramic and porcelain tile faces are non-combustible, which is why tile has become the contemporary-residential default when natural stone slab is cost-prohibitive.

### Large-format porcelain

Large-format porcelain in a slab look is one of the most-specified contemporary treatments. It delivers the visual of continuous stone at a fraction of the fabrication and material cost. Pair with the [Mezzo](/fireplaces/mezzo/) for great-room media walls where the aesthetic goal is clean and horizontal.

Specify the right substrate. The tile face is non-combustible; the cement board or backer behind it must also be confirmed non-combustible for the sections adjacent to the firebox opening. Standard drywall is not the right substrate in the firebox-adjacent zone.

### Vertical fluted tile

Fluted tile -- either in a matte glaze or an unglazed finish -- has moved from specialty to mainstream over the last two years. Vertical fluting emphasizes ceiling height, making it a natural pair for the [Cosmo Linear Gas Fireplace](/fireplaces/cosmo/). Specify with a compatible substrate and confirm grout joint integrity under thermal cycling.

### Hand-glazed specialty tile

Zellige, terracotta, and other hand-glazed specialty tiles read as high-craft choices that distinguish a residential installation from a production build. These are typically specified in smaller accent installations -- a primary bedroom fireplace feature, a powder room. The material's natural variation creates visual depth that factory tile doesn't replicate.

Confirm that any hand-glazed specialty tile is rated for the temperature exposure at the specified clearance distance. Ask the tile supplier for technical data sheets before final selection.

## Wood mantels and surrounds

Wood is the material with the most aesthetic warmth and the most engineering complexity. It is combustible. That fact governs every specification decision.

The engineering work is done. The dimensions are confirmed. The clearance-to-combustibles math is built into the product.

This is why specifying a Heat & Glo wood mantel against a Heat & Glo fireplace is different from specifying a custom wood mantel. The accessory is designed for the firebox.

- **[Oak Corbeled Mantel](/accessories/oak-corbeled-mantel/)** -- traditional profile, pairs with 6K/8K Series and True
- **Fillmore Mantel Shelf** -- transitional profile, works across fireplace families

### Custom wood paneling

Custom wood paneling on the walls adjacent to and above the firebox is a different conversation than a mantel shelf. The clearance requirements in the installation manual specify minimum distances from the firebox opening to any combustible material. Those distances must be honored regardless of how the wood is finished, sealed, or attached.

If the design calls for wood paneling that runs closer to the firebox than the installation manual permits, the material specification needs to change -- not the clearance distances.

Confirm all clearance-to-combustibles distances with a qualified installer before final design lock.

## The discipline that matters across all three materials

Every material decision flows through one fixed constraint: the clearance-to-combustibles requirements in the specific fireplace unit's installation manual.

These numbers vary by unit. A clearance that's acceptable for one model may not be acceptable for another. Generic clearance guidance from the internet, from a tile supplier, or from a design reference book is not a substitute for the unit-specific installation manual.

The sequence for any mantel specification:

1. Confirm the specific Heat & Glo unit and model number
2. Pull the installation manual for that unit
3. Identify the clearance-to-combustibles requirements for each material type
4. Design the mantel and surround to those numbers
5. Confirm with your authorized dealer and the installing contractor before material orders are placed

That sequence prevents expensive material changes after rough-in and protects the installation against code failure at inspection.

## How to choose: three questions

1. **What is the firebox family?** Contemporary linear (Mezzo, Cosmo, Primo II) pairs naturally with stone slab, large-format porcelain, or plaster. Traditional (6K/8K, True) pairs naturally with cast stone, corbeled wood mantel, or fieldstone.
2. **What are the clearance requirements for the specific unit?** Pull the installation manual before finalizing any material selection.
3. **What is the room's aesthetic?** Contemporary materials (stone slab, porcelain, plaster) read clean and modern. Traditional materials (cast stone, corbeled wood, fieldstone) read warm and hearth-forward. Transitional interiors can mix -- a stone base with a floating wood shelf is the most-specified combination in 2026.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can I put a wood mantel directly above a gas fireplace?

Only within the clearance-to-combustibles distances specified in your fireplace's installation manual. Heat & Glo wood mantel accessories are profile-engineered to compatible clearance specs. Custom wood mantels require a clearance review with your authorized dealer and installer before installation.

### What's the difference between a surround and a mantel?

A surround is the material that frames the firebox opening -- stone, tile, plaster, or brick around the perimeter of the glass. A mantel is the horizontal shelf or cap above the surround. Many installations use both: a stone or tile surround with a wood mantel cap is the most-specified combination in contemporary residential.

### Do I need a non-combustible substrate behind tile near the firebox?

Yes for the zone immediately adjacent to the firebox opening. Standard drywall is not appropriate in the firebox-adjacent area. Cement board or a verified non-combustible backer is required. Your installer and Heat & Glo dealer can confirm the required substrate for your specific unit and tile specification.

### Is plaster a viable mantel material?

Yes. Smooth and fluted plaster are non-combustible and have become a mainstream specification in contemporary residential. Plaster fluting delivers a similar vertical-emphasis effect to fluted tile at different cost. Pair with a [Cosmo](/fireplaces/cosmo/) or [Mezzo](/fireplaces/mezzo/) for a clean modern hearth.

### What stone species work best near a gas fireplace?

Most natural stone is non-combustible and performs well. Limestone, travertine, quartzite, basalt, and marble are all appropriate. The decision between them is aesthetic and budget-based, not technical. Confirm that any sealant or treatment applied to the stone is also rated for proximity to a heat source.

### How do I prevent the stone surround from cracking near the firebox?

Thermal cycling -- heating and cooling -- can stress stone surrounds if the setting materials aren't appropriate. Use mortar and grout rated for high-temperature applications in the zone closest to the firebox opening. Your installer should confirm the setting material specs before installation begins.
